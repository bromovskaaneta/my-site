01 · About me

About me
& the process.

My name is Aneta Bromovská. I study graphic and media design at the Prague College of Creative Communication (VŠKK). Serpente is the topic of my bachelor thesis.

Why this project

I have lived in Roztoky for most of my life. When the owners of a new Italian bistro mentioned they were opening near the winding road below my house, I immediately wanted to be part of it. I knew the town. I knew the building. I knew the road that gave the place its name.

The match was perfect: I needed a real client for my thesis, and the bistro needed a complete visual identity from scratch. The work was hands-on from the first day — every decision had to fit a real space, real owners, and a real opening date.

How I worked

The project was split into two parts: a theoretical analysis and an applied design. I built the theory first, so every visual decision later could be defended.

STEP 01
Theory

I researched marketing, branding, colour psychology, typography, and the retro-revival trend. This gave me the framework to make decisions, not just guesses.

STEP 02
Local research

I analysed the five competing pizzerias and bistros in Roztoky — their logos, menus, and overall feel. The local market was missing a place with a strong identity.

STEP 03
Client work

I met with Nebo and Karolína many times. We defined the brand values together. Their personal story shaped the whole project.

STEP 04
Design system

I built the identity from the inside out: values → logo → colour → typography → applications. Each part had to support the one before it.

STEP 05
Real outputs

Menu, business cards, loyalty card, packaging, uniform, exterior signage, social media, posters, and a guerrilla campaign with a Vespa on the town square.

STEP 06
Documentation

I wrote it all up in the thesis itself — what I chose, what I rejected, and why. The documentation is as important as the design.

What was difficult

The hardest part was not over-designing. It is tempting to add another texture, another illustration, another colour — but a brand identity needs restraint. Every additional element has to earn its place. I had to cut things I liked because they did not serve the brand.

The second challenge was working with a real client. The bistro is not a hypothetical case study; the design will be used. That changed how I thought about every decision — packaging had to be printable, the logo had to work in a small size on a wall, and the menu had to be readable in low light.

"Design is not what it looks like. Design is what it does." — Steve Jobs

This quote followed me through the project. Every choice — the colour, the font, the curve in the logo — had to do something for the brand, not just look nice.

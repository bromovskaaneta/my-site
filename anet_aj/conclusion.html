05 · Conclusion

What I
learned.

A bachelor thesis is not just a final mark — it is a chance to test what you can actually do. Three things became clear during this project.

01 — Theory and design belong together

I started the project thinking the theory would be the boring part to get out of the way. I was wrong. Reading about colour psychology, brand equity, and retro-revival gave me arguments, not just opinions. When I sat down with the client and had to explain why the logo should be olive green and not red, the theory was the reason I could answer with confidence.

A designer who can defend every decision earns the client's trust. The theoretical part of this thesis is what allowed me to do that.

02 — A real client changes everything

Working for a real bistro, not a hypothetical one, raised the stakes. Every decision had to survive a printer, a kitchen, a façade, and real customers. I could not hide weak ideas behind nice mockups — they had to actually work in the world.

This is the biggest difference between school projects and real design. The client does not ask "is this on trend?" — they ask "does this work for me?" Learning to design for that question is more valuable than any single visual outcome.

"Aneta, we might actually buy that Vespa."

That sentence — from Nebo after seeing the guerrilla mockup — was the moment I understood that design is not just decoration. A good visual idea can push a client toward something they wouldn't have considered on their own. Designers give clients permission to dream a little bigger.

03 — Place is an underrated brand ingredient

Most pizzeria brands try to look as "Italian" as possible — flags, cursive fonts, photos of the Colosseum. The Serpente identity goes the other way: it is rooted in one specific Czech town. The road. The forest. The oven. The local family that runs the place.

This is what makes the brand recognisable. Anyone can copy the colour or the typeface, but no one else can claim the road in Roztoky. Local identity is a competitive advantage — and it is the cheapest one to build, because it is already there. You only have to notice it.

Looking back

If I were to start over, I would do a few things differently:

LESSON 01
More client meetings, earlier

The values workshop with Nebo and Karolína was the most useful single hour of the project. I should have done it on day one, not week three.

LESSON 02
Print prototypes sooner

Some colours that looked great on screen needed adjusting on paper. Testing actual print early would have saved iteration cycles later.

LESSON 03
Cut more, not add

My natural instinct was to add more details, more colours, more illustrations. The strongest version of the brand came from cutting, not from adding.

Final thoughts

The Serpente project taught me that visual identity is not a logo, a colour, or a font — it is a set of decisions that together feel like one place. A guest who walks past the façade, opens a menu, takes home a box, and follows the Instagram account should feel the same thing every time. Building that feeling, piece by piece, is what brand design actually is.

Thank you for reading.

Ciao.

— Aneta Bromovská
